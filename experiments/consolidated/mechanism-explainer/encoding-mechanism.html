Numeric encoding · the mechanism

Why encoding a scalar can beat feeding the raw number.

An 8-dimensional nonlinear encoding lets a model read where a value sits on its axis, not just how big it is. That unlocks two things a raw / log / standardized scalar can't give — and it tells you exactly when the extra dimensions are worth it.

The setup

It hinges on the shape of the target, not the model.

A basis earns its keep only when the risk lives in a specific place on the value axis — not when it merely tracks magnitude.

  • Smooth / monotone — risk rises or falls gradually with the value; every value carries a little signal. One well-conditioned number is enough.
  • Localized (sharp) — risk spikes only inside a narrow band and is flat elsewhere. Now the model must know exactly where the value falls, not just how big it is.
  • The single property that governs everything downstream is how localized the risk is — a needle, not a hump.

The core idea

A scalar keeps “how big” and throws away “where”.

An encoding swaps magnitude for position — it reports which region of the value axis you're in.

  • A raw / log / standardized scalar is one number: magnitude. Feeding it to the model asks it to be a function of size.
  • A local basis — quantile bins (PLE), learned hinges, or RBF bumps — reports which slice of the axis the value lands in.
  • For a localized target, that location is the signal — which is why the basis, not the raw number, carries it.

Obstacle 1

A model that reads the value affinely can't form the shape.

For a linear head or a GRU gate, the per-feature function class is exactly the span of the encoding.

  • An affine read — W · e(x) before a fixed gate — can only produce shapes the encoding spans.
  • One scalar spans a single monotone line. A non-monotone or localized target isn't in that span, at any weight.
  • A local basis widens the span to include bumps, so a weighted sum reproduces the needle. This is why a basis helps affine-read models on any non-log-linear shape.

Obstacle 2

Even a model that could form it can't find it by SGD.

A free-nonlinearity MLP can represent any 1-D shape — but gradient descent can't locate a needle from a bare scalar.

  • The MLP can represent a sharp bump (universal approximation). Representability isn't the problem.
  • Optimization is. The loss over “where to put the bump” is flat everywhere except at the needle — no slope for SGD to follow, so it never lands there.
  • A fixed quantile basis hands over the location: the bins already carve the axis where the spike lives, turning “find the needle” into “read the right bin”.
  • This is why a basis helps even a free-nonlinearity model — but only for a sharp target. It is the study's refutation of the older “architecture law”.

The one account

Form and find are two faces of the same thing: localization.

A basis helps when the model can't form the shape (it isn't in the span) or can't find it by SGD (the loss is flat except at the needle). Both are ways of saying the signal lives at a specific place — and the encoding pre-supplies the places.

A basis pre-carves the value axis into local regions, so the model reads off which region — instead of bending one number into the right shape, or hunting for a needle in the dark.

The payoff

When to reach for a basis — and when not to.

Match the encoding to the shape of the risk, not to the type of model.

LEAVE IT A SCALAR

  • Monotone / log-adequate risk — a basis only imports a dimensionality tax.
  • A free-nonlinearity model on a smooth target — it forms and finds the shape unaided.
  • When the field's risk isn't localized, the extra dimensions don't pay.

ENCODE IT

  • Sharp / localized risk — helps any model, even one with a free nonlinearity.
  • Smooth non-monotone risk into an affine-read model — a learned projection.
  • A GRU gate or linear head reading the value directly — the crossover comes sooner.

Which kind of basis: for a needle, fixed PLE wins — its quantile knots are pre-placed, so there's nothing for SGD to find. For a smooth shape into an affine model, a learned projection wins — it finds smooth shapes fine and pays a smaller tax. In the study: a free-nonlinearity MLP jumped 0.13 → 0.52 PR-AUC on the sharp band with PLE; the affine GRU's PLE beat log by +0.35 on sharp (vs a projection's +0.10), and the projection led on smooth by +0.19.
